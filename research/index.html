Research

Today’s genome model is an attention-based model pretrained on billions of genes, with a classifier bolted on top. We think the models that come next will look very different, and they will need to read a genome as a living system, not a string to predict.

Sequence models learn what genomes look like, not what they do. On function and phenotype they stall: more data barely helps, and a simple probe on gene content often matches a billion-parameter encoder. Survival is organised by mechanism, not by sequence similarity, and averaging a genome into a single vector discards the very machinery that decides how an organism lives. The models that come next will treat a genome as a system to reason about rather than a string to autocomplete, grounded in its pathways, interactions, and dynamics, and learning what a genome does before asking what it is.
